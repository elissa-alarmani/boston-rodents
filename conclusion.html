Conclusion

We feel that this analysis is very important for Boston residents, policy makers, and Boston government as it provides an alternative approach at identifying inequitable government services. By analyzing 311 requests for rodent occurrences, we are able to assess the success and consistency of Boston government‚Äôs techniques for rodent prevention and mediation at the neighborhood, income, and racial level. Across some axes (Case completion time vs. income level, non-completed cases vs. income level, rodent cases per capita vs. neighborhood), Boston performed well in terms of fair treatment. However, we have preliminary results that present some disparity in rodent presence: higher white populated neighborhoods often have lower rodent presence and low income neighborhoods have a higher rate of non-completed rodent 311 cases.

However, some limitations do exist. The 311 dataset only includes reported rodent sightings, which does not account for unreported cases or systemic differences in complaint frequency across neighborhoods. Some residents may be less likely to report rodent issues due to language barriers, lack of awareness, or distrust in city services, potentially skewing the data. It is worth letting this analysis motivate inspection into animal control services, sanitation practices, and 311 reporting accessibility to ensure that rodents are not unequally bothersome for any Boston community.
