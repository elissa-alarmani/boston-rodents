Boston’s Rampant Rodents: Which neighborhoods face the most infestation?

Introduction

This project is studying the presence of rodents in Boston neighborhoods and analyzing what communities experience the most issues with them. This topic is important because there are often significant differences in cleanliness of neighborhoods and quality of living conditions in major cities, including Boston. We examine whether these differences in clean living conditions vary amongst Boston neighborhoods by race and income in order to address a larger problem of inequality in the United States. Specifically, focusing on the presence of rodents in Boston allows us to draw connections between these important issues and uncover interesting information on who experiences the most problems with rodents in the city, and what other factors may be lying beneath those findings.

Data

We used two datasets from the City of Boston’s open data portal: the 311 Service Requests dataset and the Neighborhood Demographics dataset. The 311 dataset contains over 200,000 records, but it has been cleaned to capture the 5,015 entries related to rodent activity. The Neighborhood Demographics dataset provides us with socioeconomic data like household income, racial composition, and population size. Merging these two datasets allowed for us to analyze rodent activity about socioeconomic factors in Boston, making it suitable for our analysis. It also includes geospatial data to analyze the rodent activity. By merging these datasets on the neighborhood field, we examined whether lower-income and racially marginalized communities experience disproportionately high rodent activity and slower municipal response times. More specifically, we used the features relating to percentage of a racial demographic in each neighborhood, income levels, and open and close date for case information.
